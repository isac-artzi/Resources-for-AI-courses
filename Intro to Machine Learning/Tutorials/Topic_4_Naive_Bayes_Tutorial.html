In Topic 4 you build a small spam classifier with Multinomial Naive Bayes. This page walks through Bayes' theorem in plain English with an interactive calculator, shows how Naive Bayes scores a piece of text, and explains the standard evaluation metrics.

1. Bayes' theorem in one English sentence

The probability of a class given the features is proportional to how likely the features are under that class, times how common that class is in general:

P(class | features) ∝ P(features | class) × P(class)

The proportional symbol "∝" is doing real work here. The full formula divides by P(features), but that denominator does not depend on the class — so for picking the most likely class, we can ignore it.

3. Naive Bayes for text — the spam intuition

A spam classifier looks at the words in an email and asks: is this more likely under the spam distribution of words, or the ham distribution? Each word that appears more often in spam pushes the score toward spam; each ham-leaning word pushes the other way.

Type a short email below to see a toy spam classifier in action. The numbers next to each word are per-word evidence scores: positive numbers vote for spam, negative numbers vote for ham.

4. Why "Naive" and what Laplace smoothing fixes

"Naive" Bayes assumes that, once you know the class, the words are independent. This is almost never literally true — the word "free" and the word "money" are clearly correlated — but the classifier still does well in practice.

A subtler problem: if a word appears in your test email but never appeared in any spam training email, the model's spam probability for that word is zero. Multiplying anything by zero gives zero, so one rare word would swamp the whole prediction. Laplace smoothing adds a tiny "+α" to every word count before computing probabilities. The introductory course leaves α at its default of 1.0 — no tuning required.

5. Evaluation metrics for a spam classifier

Accuracy is misleading when one class dominates (e.g., 95% ham). Always look at the confusion matrix and per-class precision/recall.

predicted hampredicted spam
actual hamTN = 450FP = 20
actual spamFN = 30TP = 30
  • Accuracy = (TP+TN)/N = 480/530 ≈ 0.906
  • Precision (spam) = TP/(TP+FP) = 30/50 = 0.60
  • Recall (spam) = TP/(TP+FN) = 30/60 = 0.50
  • F1 (spam) = harmonic mean ≈ 0.545

Read this carefully: the classifier sounds great at 91% accuracy, but it only catches half of all spam, and 40% of its spam flags are wrong. That's why we look at the matrix, not just the accuracy number.

7. TF-IDF in one diagram

Naive Bayes for text needs each document represented as a vector of numbers. The introductory pipeline uses one of:

VectorizerWhat it countsWhen to use
CountVectorizerRaw token counts per documentShort, simple text; baseline first attempt.
TfidfVectorizerTerm-frequency weighted by inverse document frequencyDiverse documents; usually a small win over counts.

TF-IDF down-weights words that appear in nearly every document ("the", "and") and up-weights words that appear often in a particular document but rarely overall (so they discriminate between classes).
