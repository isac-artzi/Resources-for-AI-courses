Topic 6 · Clustering & Dimensionality Reduction

In Topic 6 you build a small unsupervised-learning explorer with K-Means and PCA at an introductory level. This page lets you step through K-Means iterations and watch PCA compress data, so the algorithms feel less like magic and more like procedures.

1. K-Means in slow motion

K-Means is a recipe with two repeating steps:

  1. Assign each data point to its nearest cluster centroid.
  2. Update each centroid to the mean of the points assigned to it.

Repeat until nothing changes. Click Step to advance one iteration. The centroids are big diamonds; data points are small circles colored by their current assignment.

2. Choosing k: the elbow + silhouette check

The "elbow" method runs K-Means for several values of k and plots the inertia. As k grows, inertia always decreases — but the drop flattens at the natural k. The silhouette score, computed once at the chosen k, adds a per-point confirmation that points are closer to their own cluster's center than to any other cluster's.

inertia vs k (the elbow)

introductory recipe: plot inertia for k = 2…10, eyeball where the curve "bends" (the elbow), then compute one silhouette score at that k as a sanity check. Formal assumption tests are out of scope.

3. PCA: summarizing many features with a few

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) finds a few new combined features ("components") that together capture as much of the variation in your data as possible. The first component captures the most variation, the second captures the next most, and so on. The bar chart below shows the percentage of variance explained by each component for a synthetic 6-feature dataset.

The introductory rule is to keep enough components to clear a stated cumulative-variance threshold (often 80% or 95%). In the chart above, keeping the first 3 components captures — of the total variance.

What is OUT of scope: Bartlett's test of sphericity, the Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin measure, eigenvector math, and t-SNE. The introductory course relies on the cumulative-variance threshold and the scree-plot shape.

4. Stewardship: cluster labels have no ground truth

When you cluster people — employees, customers, patients — the cluster labels are an interpretive imposition. A label like "at-risk" can become a self-fulfilling prophecy if a team acts on it. At the introductory level we are deliberately small with our claims: report what the clusters appear to capture, document the choices that produced them, and refuse to label individuals with labels that were never validated against an outcome.

5. Initialization sensitivity

K-Means converges to a local minimum, not always the global one. Two different starting points can end at different clusterings — even on the same data. Click "Reroll seed" several times to see the range of outcomes. This is exactly why scikit-learn's default n_init='auto' runs the algorithm several times and keeps the best.

6. The silhouette score for individual points

The silhouette of a point is between −1 and 1. Roughly +1 means "I'm close to my cluster's center and far from the next cluster". Around 0 means "I'm on the boundary." Negative means "I'm probably in the wrong cluster." The chart below shows per-point silhouette for the K-Means demo above, grouped by cluster. Very negative bars are the points the algorithm is least confident about.

7. PCA in two dimensions — see what it does

PCA looks at the cloud of data points and finds the direction along which they spread most (PC1). The second direction (PC2) is perpendicular to the first and captures the next-most spreading direction. In two dimensions you can draw both arrows directly on the data. The percentages on the arrows are the explained-variance ratios.

Mental model: if you had to summarize each point with a single number, project it onto PC1. You will keep most of the "spread" of the data while discarding the cross-axis. That is dimensionality reduction.

8. Association rules — when does a rule "matter"?

For market-basket analysis the three numbers you compute for every rule are support (how often does this combination occur?), confidence (when the left side occurs, how often does the right side follow?), and lift (is the right side more likely after the left, compared to baseline?). Drag the sliders below to set thresholds and see which rules survive.

9. Stewardship: cluster labels are not ground truth

K-Means produced a cluster assignment for each row. Your team gave each cluster a label like "at risk" or "loyal" or "ambitious." Those labels feel like fact — but they are interpretive impositions. Nobody validated them against a real outcome.

Two habits keep this honest at the introductory level:

  1. Talk about clusters, not individuals. "Cluster 3 has lower spend on average" is fine. "Maya is in cluster 3, so Maya is at risk" is not.
  2. Report instability. If running K-Means three times with different seeds gives three different clusterings, say so in the README. Clusters that move with the seed are not robust signals.
