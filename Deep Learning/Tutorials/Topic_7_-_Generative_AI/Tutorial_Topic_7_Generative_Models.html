1 · Generative models, taxonomy math

All generative models try to learn or sample from a data distribution p(x). They differ in how they parametrize and learn it.

2 · VAE architecture, end to end mathinteractive

A VAE has three pieces:

  • Encoder qφ(z|x) — takes x, outputs the mean and log-variance of a Gaussian distribution over the latent z.
  • Latent z — sampled from that Gaussian via the reparameterization trick.
  • Decoder pθ(x|z) — takes z, outputs the parameters of a distribution over x (usually a Gaussian or Bernoulli).

3 · The VAE latent space interactive

What makes a VAE useful as a generative model is the shape of its latent space. Because the KL term in the loss pushes the posterior toward a standard normal prior, the latent space ends up roughly Gaussian — you can sample any z ~ N(0, I) and decode to a plausible x.

Demo · Drag the latent vector and watch the reconstruction change

This is a toy: the "decoder" paints a soft blob whose position and color depend on a 2-D latent z = (z₁, z₂). Real VAEs decode images, but the latent-space intuition is the same.

4 · The reparameterization trick math

The naive way to sample z from q(z|x) = N(μ, σ²) would be to call random.normal(). But random sampling is not differentiable — you can't backprop through it. The reparameterization trick fixes this by separating the randomness from the parameters:

z = μ + σ ⊙ ε, ε ∼ N(0, I)

ε is the only stochastic part; it's drawn once and fixed for that example. Gradients flow cleanly back through μ and σ to update the encoder. In PyTorch:

std    = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
eps    = torch.randn_like(std)         # the random part
z      = mu + std * eps                 # μ + σ · ε   (differentiable in μ, σ)

5 · Deriving the ELBO math

We want to maximize log p(x) but cannot compute it directly. Use Jensen's inequality on an expectation under our chosen q(z|x):

log p(x) = log ∫ p(x, z) dz
        = log ∫ q(z|x) · p(x, z) / q(z|x) dz
       ≥ E_q [ log ( p(x, z) / q(z|x) ) ]     (Jensen)
        = E_q [ log p(x|z) + log p(z) − log q(z|x) ]
        = E_q [ log p(x|z) ] − KL( q(z|x) ‖ p(z) )      ←  the ELBO

So log p(x) ≥ ELBO. Training maximizes the ELBO, which simultaneously (a) makes the reconstruction log p(x|z) high and (b) keeps the posterior q(z|x) close to the prior p(z) = N(0, I).

The negative ELBO (the actual loss) for a Gaussian decoder is:

L = reconstruction term + KL term
  = mean ((x − x̂)²)              # MSE reconstruction
  + (−1/2) · Σ (1 + log σ² − μ² − σ²)   # KL between q(z|x) and N(0, I)

6 · VAE failure modes math

  • Posterior collapse. The decoder gets so good at ignoring z that the encoder collapses q(z|x) to the prior and stops carrying any input information. Mitigation: KL annealing (start with a small KL coefficient and ramp up), more expressive decoders disabled, or a beta-VAE with explicit weighting.
  • Blurry samples. Maximizing the average reconstruction loss tends to produce "mean" outputs — sharp details get averaged away. Mitigation: use a better likelihood (mixture of logistics, normalizing flow on top), or move to a diffusion model.
  • Information bottleneck too small. A 2-dimensional latent on 64×64 images is too tight; samples become indistinguishable. Mitigation: more latent dimensions and/or hierarchical priors.

7 · DDPM forward process mathinteractive

The forward (noising) process is fixed — no parameters. At each step t, add a tiny bit of Gaussian noise according to a variance schedule βt:

q(xt | xt−1) = N(√(1−βt) · xt−1, βt · I)

After T steps, x_T is essentially pure noise. A useful closed-form jumps from x_0 directly to any x_t:

xt = √(ᾱt) · x0 + √(1 − ᾱt) · ε, ᾱt = ∏s≤t (1 − βs)

Demo · The DDPM variance schedule

Three schedules in common use. The plot shows ᾱ_t — the "fraction of signal still present" at each step. When ᾱ_t is close to 1, x_t looks like x_0; when it's close to 0, x_t is pure noise.

8 · DDPM reverse process interactive

The reverse (denoising) process is learned. Starting from x_T ~ N(0, I), the model iteratively predicts and removes noise:

pθ(xt−1 | xt) = N( μθ(xt, t), Σ )

where μθ is parametrized via a noise-prediction network εθ(xt, t). Each step subtracts a little of the predicted noise; after T steps you have a clean sample.

Demo · Watch a clean image become noise, and back

Slide t to traverse the forward (noising) process. Press Animate reverse to watch a simulated reverse trajectory recover a clean image.

9 · The simple noise-prediction loss math

In principle the DDPM loss is a variational bound similar to the VAE's ELBO. In practice you almost always train with the simple noise-prediction objective from the original DDPM paper:

L = Et, x0, ε [ ‖ ε − εθ(√(ᾱt) · x0 + √(1 − ᾱt) · ε, t) ‖² ]

In English: pick a random timestep t, pick a random training example x_0, generate random noise ε, mix them to form x_t, and ask the model to predict the noise. The MSE between predicted and actual noise is the loss. Simple, stable, and remarkably effective.

def training_step(x0, t):
    noise = torch.randn_like(x0)
    alpha_bar = ALPHA_BAR[t]
    x_t = torch.sqrt(alpha_bar) * x0 + torch.sqrt(1 - alpha_bar) * noise
    eps_pred = model(x_t, t)
    return F.mse_loss(eps_pred, noise)

10 · Sampling speed: DDPM vs DDIM mathinteractive

The original DDPM sampler needs all T denoising steps (usually 1000). That's slow at inference: 1000 forward passes per sample. DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models) reparametrizes the reverse process so you can skip steps without retraining — typically reaching comparable quality in 25-50 steps.

Demo · Quality vs sampling steps

For a fixed model, more steps usually means better samples (lower FID). DDIM lets you trade quality for speed by lowering the number of steps used at inference.

11 · Conditional generation math

To generate a particular class (e.g., "cat") instead of unconditional samples, condition the noise-prediction network on a class label c:

εθ(xt, t, c)

The clever bit (classifier-free guidance) is that at training time you randomly drop the conditioning c with some probability (e.g., 10%), so the model learns both conditional and unconditional behavior. At sampling time, mix them:

ε̂ = (1 + w) · εθ(x, t, c) − w · εθ(x, t, ∅)

Higher w ⇒ more class-faithful generations at the cost of diversity. This is the trick behind every modern image diffusion model.

12 · GAN architecture math

A GAN has two networks playing an adversarial min-max game:

  • Generator G(z) maps random noise z ~ N(0, I) to a generated sample.
  • Discriminator D(x) reads a sample (real or fake) and outputs the probability it's real.

Their losses pull in opposite directions:

LD = − Ereal log D(x) − Efake log(1 − D(G(z)))
LG = − Efake log D(G(z)) (non-saturating version)

13 · Mode collapse interactive

The most famous GAN failure mode. The generator finds a single sample (or a small cluster) that fools the discriminator and produces it for every input z. Symptom: thousands of nearly-identical generations.

15 · FID at intuition level mathinteractive

FID compares the distributions of features from a pre-trained Inception-V3 network. Fit a Gaussian to the real feature distribution and another to the generated feature distribution; compute the Fréchet (Wasserstein-2) distance between the two Gaussians:

FID = ‖μ_r − μ_f‖² + Tr( Σ_r + Σ_f − 2 · (Σ_r · Σ_f)1/2 )

Demo · Two Gaussians and their Fréchet distance

The blue cloud is "real" — fixed at N(0, I). Move the generated cloud and see FID move with it.

16 · Inception Score math

Inception Score combines two properties of the generated batch under a pre-trained Inception classifier:

IS = exp ( Ex [ KL( p(y|x) ‖ p(y) ) ] )

  • p(y|x) should be sharp: each generated image is confidently one class.
  • p(y) (the marginal) should be diverse: across the batch, all classes are represented.

Higher IS = more diverse-and-confident. Important caveats: IS only makes sense when there's a reasonable classifier for your domain (it's traditional on ImageNet-like data), and it doesn't compare your samples to the real distribution at all.

18 · Provenance manifests and watermarking interactive

Shipping a generative model without a provenance story is irresponsible. Two technical pieces, both partial mitigations:

Provenance manifest (C2PA-style)

A signed metadata blob attached to a generated sample. Verifying the signature lets a downstream consumer establish that a known signer asserted, at a particular time, the generation parameters of that sample. It does not prove that the image is harmless, that downstream copies retain integrity if cropped, or that the absence of a manifest implies the absence of synthesis.

{
  "sample_id": "a3f1...",
  "generated_at": "2026-04-29T18:24:00Z",
  "model":     { "family": "ddpm", "checkpoint_sha": "f2c4..." },
  "params":    { "seed": 1234, "denoising_steps": 200 },
  "signer":    { "alg": "ed25519", "key_id": "T7-prod" },
  "signature": "BASE64-ENCODED-ED25519-OVER-THE-CANONICAL-FIELDS"
}

Watermarking

Embed an imperceptible signal in the generated image that a downstream verifier can detect. Useful as a complement to provenance but vulnerable to common transformations (cropping, compression, re-rendering). Should be reported alongside the failure-rate of detection under realistic adversarial conditions, not just under benign conditions.

Be honest about limits. A model card for a generator should explicitly enumerate (a) what a verified manifest proves, (b) what it does NOT prove, and (c) the expected robustness of any watermark under typical post-processing. Vague claims hurt downstream consumers.
